New Orleans Market

A City That Eats for Sport

The market context

Food and culture are inseparable in New Orleans. Locals have a deeply held, multi-generational relationship with their cuisine — and they will judge every hospitality program accordingly. This is not a preference; it is a baseline expectation that operates as a test of operator credibility.

The income gap that matters

The metro median household income is $62K (census) — but Acxiom buyers average $87K. The audience is the upper half of a low-income market. 18% of metro residents live below the poverty line, compared to a national average of 12.2%. The Superdome audience is not a reflection of the city's full economic reality.

The representation gap

New Orleans' city population is 55% Black, yet only 36% of identified buyers are Black. Events like Essence Festival and the Bayou Classic close this gap intentionally — and they deserve culturally authentic F&B investment that the rest of the calendar does not always receive.

The tourism overlay

New Orleans welcomed 19.08 million visitors in 2024, spending $10.4 billion — generating nearly 40% of the city's operating budget. A meaningful slice of Superdome attendance is tourist, not local. These visitors have elevated event-day spend appetite and are looking for an authentically New Orleans experience.

Minneapolis Market

The Headquarters Economy

Quietly affluent, deeply corporate

Minneapolis doesn't announce its wealth. It accumulates it through Fortune 500 headquarters density — the Twin Cities has more Fortune 500 companies per capita than anywhere else on Earth. UnitedHealth Group, Target, General Mills, U.S. Bancorp, Cargill, 3M, Best Buy, Ameriprise: these companies are within 20 minutes of US Bank Stadium.

The census validates the ticketing data

Unlike New Orleans, where buyer income skews far above a lower-income metro, Minneapolis' $98K metro median is itself elevated — 20% above the national average. Buyer affluence here is real, not just upward self-selection from a constrained economy. The premium ceiling at US Bank is structurally supported.

The diversity nuance

The city of Minneapolis is approximately 60% White, with growing Somali, Hmong, Black, and Latino communities. Yet the buyer pool is 92% White among identified buyers. The diverse communities of Minneapolis are nearly absent from current attendance — a long-term growth opportunity that a forward-looking operator should acknowledge in their bid.

Homeownership and rootedness

The metro homeownership rate is 70.7%, well above the national average of 65.2%. Median home value hit $374K in 2024 — up 5.5% year over year. This is a stable, settled, equity-rich population with deep loyalty to their venue and high repeat-attendance rates.

Bidder Strategy

Caesars Superdome: How to Win the Bid

The one thing that matters most
Cultural authenticity is the only thing that buys trust with a New Orleans audience. Get the food right — genuinely right, not approximated — and everything else follows. Get it wrong and no amount of operational efficiency will save the contract.
✓   DO — Winning Themes
✓Lead with named New Orleans chef and restaurant partnerships — front and center in your bid, not buried in an appendix
✓Build event-specific menu programming: Essence Festival lineup ≠ Monster Jam lineup — demonstrate this granularity
✓Authentic Louisiana staples at standard concessions — boudin balls, po'boys, frozen daiquiris — as core offerings, not specialty kiosks
✓Value-architect for a $62K metro median market. Show you understand the actual city, not just the buyer sample.
✓Explicitly address the racial representation gap — name Black-owned culinary and catering partners by name
✓Show per-cap modeling by event type in your bid — it signals real market sophistication
✓Use the tourist overlay data ($10.4B annual visitor spend) to justify a premium tier pricing argument
✗   AVOID — What Sinks a Bid
✗A generic national-chain-only concession program with no local culinary identity
✗Overweighting premium at the expense of the core mid-tier audience that represents the majority of volume
✗Price points calibrated for the $87K buyer median when the real city median is $62K
✗One-size-fits-all menus that ignore the venue's profound cultural event diversity
✗Leading your bid with your corporate footprint rather than your New Orleans story
✗Treating New Orleans as interchangeable with Houston, Atlanta, or any other Southern stadium market
✗Ignoring the female-majority audience in concept development — 64% of buyers are women
Bidder Strategy

US Bank Stadium: How to Win the Bid

The one thing that matters most
The premium tier is the contract. If your suite and club program earns credibility with the corporate hospitality buyers and season-ticket holders who represent 37% of the building's affluent base, you win the economics. The rest of the building supports it — but don't mistake support revenue for the main event.
✓   DO — Winning Themes
✓Name Minneapolis restaurant partners in the bid — Manny's, Spoon and Stable, Bachelor Farmer, Union Hmong Kitchen — brand recognition matters here
✓Use "headquarters economy" language — cite Target, UHG, General Mills, U.S. Bancorp by name. This building is a corporate entertainment venue.
✓Propose a serious wine, spirits, and craft beer program that rivals a standalone restaurant — a sommelier-driven wine list, premium bourbon selections
✓Show a seasonal menu refresh cadence — signals ongoing investment. A static suite menu signals coasting.
✓Benchmark your premium offer against Minneapolis' best private dining rooms, not other stadiums
✓Integrate local craft breweries (Surly, Indeed, Summit) as a civic pride signal — this audience knows them
✓Propose a loyalty recognition program for long-term season-ticket holders — 70% homeownership signals a settled, returnable audience
✗   AVOID — What Sinks a Bid
✗A premium program that doesn't name local culinary partners — sounds imported and generic
✗Static suite menus that haven't changed in two years — the corporate client who returns quarterly will notice
✗Underinvesting in wine and spirits relative to beer — this audience is past the domestic light lager phase
✗Ignoring the value tier entirely — Monster Jam (20,914 buyers) and MSHSL events demand it
✗Treating Minneapolis' growing diverse communities as invisible in your bid narrative
✗Benchmarking only against other stadiums — this audience's frame of reference is Minneapolis restaurants
✗Underestimating the corporate entertainment use case — a significant portion of premium spend is on company accounts
Key Takeaways

What Every Hospitality Bidder Must Know

01
New Orleans

Authenticity is currency. New Orleans' food culture is older than the country. A hospitality program that approximates Louisiana cuisine will be noticed — and penalized. The city's majority-Black community is underrepresented in current attendance; closing that gap is both a cultural responsibility and a revenue opportunity.

02
Minneapolis

The premium tier is the product. Minneapolis is the most corporate-dense metro per capita in the world. US Bank Stadium is a client entertainment venue for Fortune 500 companies headquartered within 20 minutes. The suite and club program must be built to that standard — not a stadium standard.

03
Both venues

Ticket buyers are not the full market. At both venues, buyers earn $25–27K more than their metro median. The census data reveals who actually lives in each market — and those populations are more diverse, more economically constrained, and more culturally complex than the buyer sample alone suggests.

04
Both venues

Event-aware programming separates winners from losers. Essence Festival is not Monster Jam. Morgan Wallen is not Kendrick Lamar. A single menu cannot serve all of these crowds equally. The operator who demonstrates event-specific F&B thinking in their bid will outperform the one who doesn't.
